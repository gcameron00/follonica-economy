The economy, in detail

How Follonica makes a living

Three economic levels, a handful of industries and one very seasonal calendar. Below, the structure of the local economy — and the iron-age history that still shapes it.

Economic levels

Primary, secondary, tertiary

Economists sort activity into levels — extracting, making and serving. Follonica's mix leans hard toward the tertiary, a common story on the Tuscan coast.

Primary · ~6%

Extracting & growing

Maremma olive groves and vineyards, honey, market gardens and a small artisanal fishing fleet. Increasingly monetised through agritourism rather than raw output.

Secondary · ~19%

Making & building

Construction, metal and mechanical workshops, boat maintenance, food processing. The direct descendant of the town's foundry DNA.

Tertiary · ~75%

Serving & hosting

Hotels, campsites, restaurants, bars, beach concessions, retail, real-estate and public services. This is the modern engine of Follonica.

The seasonal machine

An economy with a summer heartbeat

Follonica's defining economic fact is seasonality. Population and spending swell in July and August, then fall back — a rhythm that shapes hiring, housing and public budgets.

Why it matters

A four-fold summer swing means beach concessions, restaurants and shops must earn a year's margin in a season, while roads, water and waste must be sized for the peak but paid for year-round.

The quiet months

Extending the season — spring cycling, autumn food events, off-peak wellness — is the single biggest lever local planners reach for. You can pull that lever yourself in the game.

Historical context

Two hundred years, two economies

Follonica is unusual: an Italian coastal town that was industrial before it was touristic. Its structure flipped almost completely inside a century.

  • 1577–1700s

    Medici-era ironworking uses local water and forest charcoal near the coast; the area is thinly settled and malarial.

  • 1830s

    Grand Duke Leopold II of Lorraine invests heavily in the Regie Fonderie. Iron ore from Elba is smelted here; a company town takes shape.

  • 1838

    The Church of San Leopoldo is completed with a cast-iron portico and fittings — the foundry showing off in its own building material.

  • late 1800s

    Under the new Italian state the works pass to private and national operators; Follonica is, above all, an iron town.

  • 1900s

    Land reclamation tames the marshes; rail and roads improve; the beach and pinewood begin to draw visitors.

  • mid-1900s

    Heavy iron production winds down. The industrial monoculture that built the town no longer sustains it.

  • 1960s–1990s

    Mass beach tourism and second-home building remake the economy around summer.

  • 2013

    The MAGMA museum opens inside the old furnace hall — heritage becomes a tourism asset in its own right.

About the numbers

Data note & sources

This is an educational explainer, not an official statistical release. The charts use rounded, representative estimates assembled to illustrate the shape of Follonica's economy — the direction and rough magnitude of things — rather than to report exact, audited figures.

They draw on the kinds of public sources you would consult for the real numbers:

  • ISTAT — Italy's national statistics institute (population, census, employment by sector).
  • Comune di Follonica — municipal open data, tourism and planning documents.
  • Regione Toscana / Toscana Notizie — regional tourism and economic statistics.
  • Parco Nazionale delle Colline Metallifere (Tuscan Mining UNESCO Global Geopark) and MAGMA — for the mining and foundry history.
